This is an informal, group study style course. The goal is to assemble an active group doing Data Science problems. We hope to attrach people (graduate students, postdocs, faculty, and good undergraduates) from around the different departments, etc. on campus who are interested in

  • analyzing interesting, complex and/or large data, gaining,
  • getting practice with data technologies,
  • learning new statistical/machine learning approaches and methods,
  • implementing computational solutions for the analyses,
  • and creating compelling visualizations of the information.

    Structure of the Explorations

    We plan to focus the activities around two or more specific data analysis problems. We'll introduce the general questions and the data and discuss different issues and possible approaches. Over the week, we'll work in groups to work on exploring the data and addressing the questions, discussing issues on Piazza. During the meeting each week, we'll discuss some of the more complex issues and introduce new statistical or computational techniques as they rise. These might be neural networks or boosting on the statistical side, or map reduce/hadoop for parallel computing, or a particular R package or Java library on the computational side.

    At the end of the quarter, the groups will present their best results. We'll give prizes to the best approaches and answers.

    We strongly encourage people who are facing Data Sciences challenges in their research to come and present these to the group, hopefully to educate each other, get suggestions as to how to solve some of the challenges, and explore possibilities for accessing different sources of data (e.g. Web scraping, APIs) or managing data. We'd love to hear about these and help.

    Topics

    As we say, the focus is on authentic data analysis and statistical reasoning. The focus is less on specific statistical techniques or comuting technologies. However, some of the topics we will use and/or introduce may include
    • parallel computing (in R, Python, Map Reduce, Hadoop, C, GPUs)
    • statistical/machine learning techniques (e.g. Boosting, Bagging, classification trees, random forests, logistic regression, neural networks, deep learning).
    • visualization (static and interactive Web-based visualization)

    Prerequisites/Background

    Basically, we want people who are motivated, interested, have initiative and some independence and who want to learn more Data Science skills. You should
    • have reasonable programming skills in a language such as R, Python, MATLAB, Java, C, C++.
    • have sufficient knowledge of statistics to understand heuristic descriptions of new methods (at the level of Introduction to Statistical Learning with Applications in R).
    • be willing to participate, speak up, explore data and write code.

    Sample Data Problems

  • Understand the performance of runners.
    This involves data matching (across races) and fusion as well as statistical techniques.
  • Classify a collection of pictures as either containing a dog or a cat
    This involves some image processing as well as statistical/machine learning.

    • Course Goals

    • Enhance skills in how to map a question into a statistical framework and identify and learn appropriate methods for estimation and prediction.
    • Further learn computational techniques to explore, visualize and fit models with data.
